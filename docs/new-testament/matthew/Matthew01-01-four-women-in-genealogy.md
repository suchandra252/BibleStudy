# <span style="color: #2b6cb0; font-weight: bold;">Matthew 1:1–10 || The Four Women in the Genealogy of Jesus</span>
<span style="color: #4a5568;">Zac Poonen – Verse-by-Verse Study (Matthew 1:1–10)</span>

---

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">Introduction</span>
<span style="color: #718096;">Matthew 1:3–6</span>

One of the most surprising features in Matthew’s genealogy is the mention of **four women**. In ancient Jewish genealogies women were almost never named. Even more striking is the background of these four women — each carried a story of shame, scandal, or outsider status.

Jesus, the only Person who ever chose the family, place, and time of His birth, deliberately chose to come through this broken line.

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">1. Tamar</span>
<span style="color: #718096;">Matthew 1:3 · Genesis 38</span>

> <span style="color: #e53e3e; font-weight: bold;">“Judah was the father of Perez and Zerah by Tamar.”</span>

Tamar was Judah’s daughter-in-law. After her husbands died, Judah failed to give her his third son as the law required. In desperation she disguised herself as a prostitute, sat by the roadside, and conceived twin sons through her own father-in-law.

This is a story of deception, sexual immorality, and family failure. Yet God placed Tamar in the direct line of the Messiah.

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">2. Rahab</span>
<span style="color: #718096;">Matthew 1:5 · Joshua 2 & 6</span>

> <span style="color: #e53e3e; font-weight: bold;">“Salmon was the father of Boaz by Rahab.”</span>

Rahab was a prostitute living in Jericho. When the Israelite spies came, she hid them, lied to the king’s men, and later hung a scarlet cord from her window as a sign of faith. After the fall of Jericho she was spared and married Salmon, becoming the mother of Boaz.

A Canaanite woman with a publicly immoral life was brought into the covenant people of God and into the ancestry of Christ.

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">3. Ruth</span>
<span style="color: #718096;">Matthew 1:5 · Book of Ruth · Genesis 19:30–37</span>

> <span style="color: #e53e3e; font-weight: bold;">“Boaz was the father of Obed by Ruth.”</span>

Ruth was a Moabite. The Moabites themselves originated from the incestuous relationship between Lot and his older daughter after the destruction of Sodom (Genesis 19). Under the Law, a Moabite was not allowed to enter the assembly of the Lord even to the tenth generation (Deuteronomy 23:3).

Yet this Gentile widow left her people, clung to Naomi, and became the great-grandmother of King David — and an ancestor of Jesus.

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">4. Bathsheba</span>
<span style="color: #718096;">Matthew 1:6 · 2 Samuel 11</span>

> <span style="color: #e53e3e; font-weight: bold;">“David was the father of Solomon by the wife of Uriah.”</span>

Matthew does not even use her name. He simply calls her “the wife of Uriah.” David saw her bathing, committed adultery with her, and then arranged the murder of her faithful husband Uriah the Hittite. Later she became the mother of Solomon.

A story of royal lust, adultery, deception, and murder stands in the genealogy of the Son of God.

---

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">Theological Significance</span>

Jesus deliberately chose this family line. He could have selected the most pure and dignified ancestry in Israel, but He did not.

He came through Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, and Bathsheba because:

- He came **to save sinners**, not the self-righteous (Matthew 9:13).
- He came for the least and the lowest.
- He wanted to show that no background is too shameful for the grace of God.
- He humbled Himself to the lowest place so that He could lift the lowest people.

This is the heart of the gospel.

---

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">Key Spiritual Lessons</span>

- Pride in family background, caste, education, or social status has no place in the life of a disciple.
- God can take the most broken stories and weave them into His redemptive plan.
- The Messiah fully identified with the weakness and failure of humanity.
- Anyone who still feels superior to others because of their background can never be a true disciple of Jesus.

---

## <span style="color: #2c7a7b; font-weight: bold;">Personal Application</span>

- Do I still take pride in my family name, caste, or social standing?
- Do I look down on people with a “shameful” past or background?
- Am I willing to identify with the weak and the sinful the way Jesus did?
- Have I allowed the grace of God that reached these four women to shatter every form of superiority in my heart?
